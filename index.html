🇰🇪 Kenya Youth Unemployment Analysis (1991–2025)

A data science research project by Abdifatah Muhlar

About This Project

This project analyses 35 years of Kenyan youth unemployment data to understand whether the crisis is structural or cyclical, and projects trends through 2030 using statistical forecasting. It was built independently as part of my data science learning journey — using real World Bank data, Python, and standard research methods to investigate a problem that directly affects millions of young Kenyans including myself.

Research Question

How has youth unemployment in Kenya evolved over time, and can we forecast its future trajectory using time series modeling?

Key Findings

Youth unemployment rose from 6.21% in 1991 to 15.25% in 2025 — a 145% increase over 34 years.
Trend strength = 0.983 — 98.3% of variation is explained by the structural trend, not seasonal patterns.
ARIMA forecasting projects rates reaching 16.41% by 2030 under business-as-usual conditions.
Even the most optimistic scenario (lower confidence bound) shows no improvement below current levels through 2030.

Chart 1 — Long-Term Trend

Economic Interpretation: The sustained upward trend reflects jobless growth — Kenya's GDP has expanded without generating proportional employment for young people. Three structural forces drive this: youth population pressure, skills mismatch between education and labour market demands, and an economy heavily dependent on agriculture and the informal sector.

Chart 2 — Year-on-Year Change

Economic Interpretation: Red bars represent years where unemployment increased, blue bars where it decreased. Notable spikes coincide with the Global Financial Crisis (2008–2009) and COVID-19 pandemic (2020–2022), confirming Kenya's youth employment is highly vulnerable to external economic shocks.

Chart 3 — Decade Comparison

Economic Interpretation: Each decade tells a distinct story — from structural adjustment in the 1990s, to jobless growth in the 2000s, to population surge outpacing economic growth in the 2010s, to pandemic amplification of structural weakness in the 2020s. The 2020s average of 14.92% is more than double the 1990s average of 6.55%.

Chart 4 — ARIMA Forecast (2026–2030)

Forecast Interpretation: The ARIMA(1,1,1) model projects continued deterioration through 2030. The shaded region represents the 95% confidence interval — widening over time to reflect increasing uncertainty. This forecast is a baseline warning under business-as-usual conditions, not a definitive prediction.

Policy Recommendations

  1. Invest in digital skills training — align education curricula with tech sector demand
  2. Support youth entrepreneurship — reduce regulatory and financial barriers to self-employment
  3. Strengthen vocational education — create structured pathways outside formal university
  4. Target post-crisis recovery — implement youth-specific economic stimulus during downturns
  5. Use data-driven policy monitoring — track unemployment indicators to measure intervention effectiveness
